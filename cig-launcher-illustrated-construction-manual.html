Building the Launcher

The complete first mechanical build — every stage with its proportional diagram and its step-by-step together in one place. Follow in order; each stage ends with a checkpoint to pass before the next. Blue = dimension (mm), red = an action (cut, drill, deburr).

Golden rule: build and fully tune the mechanical throw by hand before any servo touches it. A servo only replaces your finger — if the throw isn't consistent by hand, electronics won't fix it. Stages A–F are that core; G onward is the rest of the launcher.

Audit summary

  • Dimensions cross-check consistent across parts sheet, diagrams, and steps.
  • Corrections folded in: magazine perpendicular to the barrel; sear self-latching ramp; the servo trips but never holds the spring; a mounting bracket carries the sear pivot (it doesn't float).
  • Bench-tuned, not final: plunger-to-bore fit, spring rate, launch angle, servo setpoints. The single-drop feed gate is the fiddliest part — build it last.

A

Barrel — cut to length & slot

tube · cutoff wheel / tube cutter · files · sandpaper
  1. Take the ~9 mm-bore brass tube. Mark 180 mm; wrap tape at the mark with its edge on the line as a square cutting guide.
  2. Cut it — rotary cutoff wheel (rotate the tube as you go), tube cutter, or hacksaw along the tape. Keep it square.
  3. Deburr — do not skip. Cutting leaves a sharp lip that catches the plunger. File the outer edge, then clear the inner burr with a larger drill bit spun by hand or a round file.
  4. Smooth the bore: roll 400–600 sandpaper on a dowel and run it through a few times so the inside is slick.
  5. Cut the slot on top, 45 × 3 mm starting ~30 mm from the breech: drill a hole at each end, join with the wheel or a needle file, deburr.
Tip: the time spent deburring and slicking the bore is the single biggest factor in whether the whole mechanism works.
Checkpoint: a 180 mm tube, square ends, clean slot, bore smooth enough that a snug dowel slides freely.
B

Plunger — cut & drill the pin hole

nesting tube/dowel · drill + Ø2 bit · sandpaper
  1. Cut the next-smaller nesting tube (or a dowel sized to the bore) to 25 mm; deburr.
  2. Test the fit: it should drop through the bore under its weight, no rattle. Loose → tape shim or step up a size; tight → sand.
  3. Drill the pin hole: dimple ~6 mm from the back end, clamp, and drill Ø2 mm through both walls, perpendicular.
  4. Slide it into the bore; confirm the hole lines up under the slot.
Watch: support a tube plunger while drilling (a snug dowel inside, or soft vise jaws) so it doesn't crush. Go slow.
Checkpoint: plunger slides free, and its Ø2 hole aligns with the barrel slot.
C

Cross-pin & spring — fit into the breech

Ø2 rod/nail · epoxy · spring assortment
  1. Cut the Ø2 rod to ~25 mm. Push it through the plunger so it stands up ~15 mm, and lock it with a dab of epoxy.
  2. Pick the weakest spring (~Ø8) and drop it into the bore behind the plunger — you'll swap springs when tuning, so don't fix it yet.
  3. Slide the assembly in with the pin up through the slot; check it rides the full ~30 mm travel freely. Cap the breech end so the spring pushes off something solid.
Checkpoint: pin rides the slot smoothly, and pushing the plunger back compresses the spring and it pushes back.
D

Sear — cut the hook, ramp & pivot

thin metal / 3 mm acrylic · cutoff wheel · files · Ø3 drill
  1. Cut the L-shaped hook (~32 mm arm, 12 mm foot) from 3 mm steel or acrylic.
  2. Ramp the muzzle-facing face of the foot — this is what lets the pin cam it up and re-latch on the way back (self-latching).
  3. Drill the Ø3 pivot hole near the far end of the arm.
Checkpoint: the hook fits over the cross-pin cleanly, and the ramped face slopes toward the muzzle.
E

Sear — mount the bracket

baseplate · L-bracket / post · Ø3 screw + nut · return band
  1. Mount the whole launcher (barrel on saddles) to a flat baseplate so everything shares a rigid base.
  2. Stand an L-bracket / post up from the baseplate beside the barrel, next to the slot, and put the Ø3 pivot screw through its top so the hook drops onto the pin.
  3. Position the pivot so the foot lands on the pin at the cocked spot, and the post stands clear of the pin's travel.
  4. Add a light return band pulling the hook down onto the pin so it re-catches on its own.
Critical: the bracket is structural — cocked spring force runs pin → hook → pivot → bracket → baseplate. If it flexes or isn't anchored, the hook creeps and won't hold. Sturdiest: two side plates straddling the barrel with the screw through both.
Checkpoint (by hand): pull the plunger back → the hook self-latches over the pin and holds cocked. Lift the hook → it fires under spring force.
F

How it cocks — the self-latch

by hand

Cocking is manual in this build: pull the plunger (or a handle on the pin) back until it clicks. The ramped hook does the latching for you — the pin cams it up on the way back, then it drops in front of the pin and traps the compressed spring on the sear. Let go; it stays cocked. To fire, the servo (or your finger) lifts that hook.

Checkpoint: pull → click → stays cocked with the spring held on the sear. Lift the hook → fires.
G

Assemble the core & tune the throw — the big one

adjustable prop / stand · cigarettes
  1. Assemble breech → muzzle: spring · plunger+pin · loaded cigarette. Prop the barrel at ~30–45°, muzzle up, aimed at your hand ~1–1.5 m away.
  2. Cock it and fire by lifting the sear with your finger.
  3. Tune the spring: crushes or overshoots → go weaker; drops short → slightly stiffer. Aim for a gentle lob, not a snap.
  4. Tune the angle until a cigarette lands soft in your open palm. Repeat until it's boring — same spot, shot after shot.
Green light: cock → release → cigarette lands in your hand repeatably, same place, no crushing, many times. Only now add the servo. If it's not consistent, fix it here first.
H

Release servo — the linkage

servo · bracket/hot glue · stiff pushrod (paperclip/RC rod)
  1. Mount the release servo near the pivot (bracket or hot glue).
  2. Link the horn to the sear's tail with a stiff pushrod (bent paperclip / RC rod, Z-bend each end) so rotating the servo lifts the hook.
  3. Wire signal → GPIO4; set release_rest (hook held down) and release_trip (~20° swing that clears the pin). Test with the BOOT button, then "cig me."
Tip: leave a little slack at rest so the servo isn't pressing on the sear — the return band holds the hook, the servo only takes up slack when it fires. That keeps the spring load off the servo entirely (the sear holds it).
Checkpoint: "cig me" (or BOOT) → servo lifts the sear → a cigarette fires. First full end-to-end shot.
I

Magazine & feed — perpendicular, single-drop

3 mm acrylic/ply · feed servo · gate plate
  1. Cut a column ~86 mm internal (cig length + clearance), depth ~10 mm, holding cigarettes parallel to the barrel, stacked like a roll of coins.
  2. Mount it perpendicular to the barrel over the breech load point — this keeps every cigarette bore-aligned, and it still gravity-feeds at 45°.
  3. Make the load region an open-topped trough so the bottom cigarette drops into open bore ahead of the retracted plunger and settles on its face.
  4. Add a gate (a sliding plate with a one-cigarette pocket) driven by the feed servo (signal → GPIO5); tune the throw (~1 Ø) so exactly one drops. Keep the stack short (4–6), walls slick, drop hole chamfered.
Don't stand the magazine straight up — a cigarette must arrive parallel to the tight bore or it jams. Perpendicular is the geometry that works.
Tip: the single-drop gate is the fiddliest mechanism — expect several rounds tuning pocket depth and throw. Build it last.
Checkpoint: each feed cycle drops one and only one cigarette, which settles on the plunger face.
J

Auto-reload switch

lever microswitch · wire
  1. Mount a lever microswitch at the breech so it's pressed when the plunger is pulled fully back (re-cocked).
  2. Wire it: one leg → GPIO6, the other → GND. The firmware runs the feed when it closes.
  3. Full cycle: say "cig me" → fires → pull back to re-cock → the switch trips → the next cigarette drops → ready. (Until you add the switch, drop one in by hand.)
Checkpoint: fire, re-cock, and the reload happens automatically and correctly sequenced (fire → re-cock → feed).
K

Mount — 40° wedge & wall cleat

3 mm ply · French cleat · wall anchors · driver
  1. Build the ~40° wedge base and fix the launcher to it, keeping the magazine on its correct (perpendicular) angle.
  2. Attach half a French cleat to the back; mount the mating half on the wall at reachable height (reach the magazine + cocking handle), near an outlet.
  3. Hang it, aim the muzzle at your catch spot, and confirm it lifts off cleanly for reloading and re-cocking.
Done: mounted, powered, aimed. Say "cig me," catch, re-cock, repeat.
